My research is focused in the structures and networks that underlie associative memory, and more specifically, the mechanisms that allow us to remember how, where and when things that are important for our life happened, and how we change our responses when the previous learned ones are no longer valid. These two phases, called retrieval and extinction, allow us to adapt to our context and to survive. We do it multiple times a day, but those mechanisms fail in case of cognitive dysfunctions, like early phases of a dementia. I try to elucidate the neurobiological basis of these phases both in physiological conditions, and as early detection markers for cognitive dysfunctions.

I studied Biology in Oviedo University, where I finished afterwards my Masters in Neuroscience Research. I completed my International Doctorate in “Structures and networks underlying Extinction and retrieval” under the Supervision of Prof. Nélida Conejo, in the Institute for Neurosciences, Principality of Asturias, with short stays both in the Laboratory of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics, Institute of Biomedical Research in Light and Image (IBILI), University of Coimbra (Portugal) and the Sandler Centre for Integrative Neuroscience, UCSF, San Francisco (USA). Since 2016 I am a postdoc researcher in the Neurophysiology department and Postdoc of the 04 Subproject for SFB1280 Extinctions learning project, under the supervision of Prof. Denise Manahan-Vaughan.

I am currently working in the Neural mechanism of extinction Learning. This project focus on the neurophysiological basis of extinction learning, particularly at the (sub)cellular levels of encoding and processing within neuronal populations.  We use both molecular biology and electrophysiological approaches to examine the neural mechanisms and signatures of task acquisition, extinction learning and renewal in the rodent brain.  We focus on the hippocampus and its interactions with the Prefrontal cortex and their relationships with the rest of the limbic system. In this project we also want to understand up to what extent neuromodulatory structures and plasticity-related proteins contribute to these processes.
